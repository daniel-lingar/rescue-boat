# PART II: TECHNICAL APPENDIX

## Deep Science and Reframes

### 1. Neurobiology of Withdrawal – GABA & Glutamate

Alcohol and benzodiazepines work on GABA, the brain's main inhibitory neurotransmitter – the brake pedal. Chronic use causes the brain to turn down its own GABA production. When you stop, the glutamate (excitatory) system runs unchecked. No brakes, just gas.

This is why withdrawal can cause seizures, tremors, and the feeling of being "on fire." It's not punishment. It's physics.

**Sources:**
- NIDA (brain disease model)
- Maté, *In the Realm of Hungry Ghosts* (2005)
- Khantzian, *The Self-Medication Hypothesis* (1997)

### 2. Savior vs. Co-Regulator – Internalization

Trauma survivors often seek a savior – someone to fix them, carry their pain, never leave. This leads to dependency and repeated abandonment.

Co-regulation is different. A co-regulator offers a steady pulse – a nervous system that stays calm enough for you to borrow that calm. Over time, you internalize that steadiness. You learn to regulate yourself.

**Sources:**
- Porges, *Polyvagal Theory* (2022)
- van der Kolk, *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014)

### 3. Monster vs. Mechanic – Identity Reframe

**Monster:** shame-based identity. "I am bad at my core." Leads to hiding, isolation, helplessness.

**Mechanic:** trauma-informed identity. "I am a machine built on shaky ground. I never got a manual." Leads to tool-seeking, repair, growth.

The shift from monster to mechanic is the key to lasting recovery.

**Sources:**
- Brown, *I Thought It Was Just Me* (2007)
- Felitti, *ACE Study* (1998)

### 4. The Body's Score – Physical Symptoms

Unprocessed trauma shows up in the body: ulcers, migraines, sleep paralysis, night terrors, chronic muscle tension, sensory hypersensitivity. "The body keeps score." These symptoms are not separate medical issues. They are the body's language of unspoken pain.

**Sources:**
- van der Kolk, *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014)
- Levine, *Waking the Tiger* (1997)

### 5. At Least 25 Names – Pattern Recognition, Not Paranoia

When you lose at least twenty-five people you love, your brain learns a pattern: tragedy is the rule, not the exception. Expecting the worst is not "catastrophizing" – it's data-driven prediction. The goal is to honor the data without being destroyed by it.

**Sources:**
- Bonanno, *The Other Side of Sadness* (2009)
- Seligman, *Learned Optimism* (1990)
