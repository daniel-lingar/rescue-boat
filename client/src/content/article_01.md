# The Rescue Boat

> Addiction is survival, not weakness.

For forty-three years, I believed I was an addict. That was the label. That was the verdict. Doctors gave me other labels too – bipolar, borderline, depressed – but the one that burned deepest was addict. I believed it because everyone around me believed it. My ex-wife said I chose drugs over my kids. The system said I was noncompliant, self-destructive, unwilling to change. I said it to myself in the dark: You're weak. You're selfish. You'd rather get high than be a father.

Here's what I know now, after digging through the wreckage of my own nervous system. I wasn't choosing drugs over my family. I was choosing not to die, with the only shitty tools I had.

## The Question Nobody Asks

Society loves to blame the boat. "You're just an addict." "Weak." "Selfish." "Why can't you just stop?"

But nobody ever asks why the boat launched in the first place. Nobody wants to look at the storm that made the water so rough you needed anything – anything – to keep from drowning.

Let me tell you about my storm. I was born three pounds, four ounces, dropped into a glass box of wires and alarms. Thirty days in an incubator. No mother's touch. No skin-to-skin. Just machines and a priest giving me last rites because I was dying. My body learned distance before my mind knew the word. It learned that danger is normal, that peace is the exception, that the alarm never shuts off.

Then came the blanket night. Age six, huddled under a thin blanket while my mother got beaten. If I move, I'd be next. That lesson got written into my nervous system like a line of code: freeze keeps you alive. Don't move. Don't cry. Don't exist.

By the time I was a teenager, my baseline wasn't calm – it was panic. My heart raced in quiet rooms. My jaw clenched for no reason. I scanned every room for exits. Fluorescent lights buzzed like NICU monitors inside my skull.

That's not anxiety in the cute, manageable way people talk about it. That's a nervous system carved by repeated, inescapable threat, and I've since learned that this pattern has a name. Researchers call it neuroadaptation – the brain building a chemical scaffolding just to survive. The earliest environments, like a neonatal ICU, can recalibrate the stress response for life. The experts have shown that the body keeps the score, that adversity retunes the nervous system to treat friends as enemies, that trauma literally rearranges the brain's wiring.

When that alarm won't shut off – when you can't take another hour of the screaming in your head – you reach for anything that dials it down long enough to breathe. That's the rescue boat.

## The Rescue Boat Is Not the Enemy

At sixteen, I found alcohol. It dulled the shakes. Made me loose. Gave me a few hours of quiet inside my own skull. At seventeen, a migraine sent me reaching for a pill. Hydrocodone. Thirty minutes later, I was at the sink scrubbing dishes like I was reborn. Not high. Not euphoric. Just… normal. And normal was the most addictive state I'd ever chased, because my baseline had never been anything close to it.

By nineteen, I wasn't using to escape. I was using to operate. Pills got me through double shifts. Meth kept me upright when grief would have put me on the floor. I learned the math of maintenance: how many milligrams to function, how to keep your voice steady when your pupils pin and your hands shake. I wasn't living. I was running a machine on borrowed fuel.

> You don't choose addiction. You choose the nearest thing that turns down the volume.

The substance isn't the disease. It's a symptom. A desperate, clumsy, dangerous attempt to regulate a nervous system that never learned what safety felt like. The research backs this – the self-medication hypothesis, the polyvagal theory, the ACE study – but you don't need the footnotes to know it's true. You've lived it. Every time you picked up, every time you used, every time you reached for something to turn down the noise – that wasn't weakness. That was your nervous system screaming: "I want to live. I just don't know how anymore."

Does that excuse the damage I caused? No. I hurt my kids. I burned bridges. I own that. But calling addiction a moral failure doesn't help. It just adds shame on top of the fire. And shame makes everything worse.

## The Shame Spiral

Here's what happens when you treat the rescue boat as the enemy.

You use. You feel relief. The alarm quiets for a few hours. Then the substance wears off. The alarm comes back louder than ever – because your nervous system has been suppressed, not healed. And on top of the original pain, you now have shame. You're weak. You're an addict. You chose this. You're hurting everyone you love.

The shame is so heavy that the only thing you know to do is use again. Just to get through the night. Just to stop the screaming for one more hour. That's the spiral. And it kills people slowly, quietly, one relapse at a time.

I've buried friends who got caught in that spiral. They weren't bad people. They weren't weak. They were drowning in a storm they never asked for, and the shame told them they deserved it.

## What I Learned When I Stopped Asking the Wrong Question

For years, I asked: "Why am I so fucked up?" That question is shame-based. It assumes brokenness. It looks for evidence to confirm the guilty verdict.

Then a therapist looked me in the eye and said: "It's not what's wrong with you. It's what happened to you." I started asking a different question: "How did I survive this wiring long enough to still be here?" That question changed everything.

I wasn't an addict because I was weak. I was an addict because my nervous system was screaming for relief and I had no tools, no map, no manual. The substance was the only thing I'd found that could turn down the volume. That's not a moral failure. That's a predictable response to an impossible situation.

The rescue boat was never the enemy. Even now, I still fight the urge to reach for something when the alarm blares. Some days I win. Some days I don't. But I don't call myself a failure anymore. I call myself a machine still learning new code. And if you're still reaching for your own rescue boat, hear this: you're not weak. You're trying to live. The only thing missing is other tools.

So here's the one that helps me. When the urge hits – the pull toward the thing that quiets the noise – I don't fight it with shame. I name it, soft, so only I can hear: "That's my nervous system asking for peace. I can find other tools." You can borrow my words if you need them.

The storm wired us for danger. The fire taught us to burn. But the boat? It was never the enemy. It was proof I still wanted to live.
